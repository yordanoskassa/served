Engineering note (B) — not part of the UI, delete before shipping
The CANNOT_CONFIRM explanation must be scenario-aware: the verdict stays rule-based (P0-SPEC §2), but the model (GPT-5.6 API) generates the explanation from whatever the lookup actually found. At minimum three sub-scenarios:
S1 — No docket match at all → the copy shown above.
S2 — Exact number misses, but a party-name search finds a near-match (same parties, number differs by a digit or two) → say so explicitly: "I couldn't find case 5:25-cv-92108, but a case with these exact parties exists under 5:25-cv-02108 — the number on your document may be a typo." Show the candidate docket as evidence. Verdict stays CANNOT_CONFIRM — the candidate is a lead for the attorney, never auto-confirmed.
S3 — The number exists but belongs to a different case (parties don't match) → state that plainly; strong reason for attorney review.
All three end in the same CTA (route to human). Note: demo document D2 is exactly the S2 case — its true sibling (5:25-cv-02108, Barnes v. Maximus Consulting Services) is live in RECAP, so the demo's refusal shot can show the near-match recovery.

Scam indicators

This matches known scam patterns

This letter is not from any real court. It matches three known patterns of impersonation scams that target business owners. Do not pay. Do not call the number on the letter.
The three signals found
#1"Federal Legal Processing Bureau" is not a real court — no such body exists in the federal directory
#2Demands payment in gift cards ("$2,400 in Google Play cards") — no court or agency ever does this
#648-hour threat deadline — real legal process gives statutory notice periods, not countdowns
What to do
1Keep the letter — it's evidence.
2Report it at reportfraud.ftc.gov — it takes two minutes.
3If you already paid, contact your bank immediately.
A scam verdict requires two or more affirmative fraud signals, each cited above. Absence from the court record alone is never called a scam.
